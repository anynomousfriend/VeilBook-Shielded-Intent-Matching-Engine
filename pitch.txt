VEILBOOK — The First Trustless Dark Pool on Blockchain
The Problem Nobody Has Solved
Every day, $200 billion moves through dark pools in traditional finance. Why? Because when you place a large order on a public exchange, the market sees it before it executes. Bots front-run you. Prices move against you. Your strategy is exposed to every competitor watching the order book.

Crypto made this worse, not better. On Ethereum, Solana, every public DEX — your pending transactions sit in a mempool for the world to see. MEV bots extracted $900M+ in 2023 alone by front-running, sandwiching, and exploiting transparent order flow.

The industry's answer? "Use a centralized dark pool." Which defeats the entire point of decentralization. You're just trusting a different intermediary.

Until now, the choice has been: privacy OR trustlessness. Pick one.

Explain It Like I'm 12: The Sealed Envelope Trading Game
Imagine you and your friend both want to trade Pokemon cards, but you're in a school cafeteria full of kids who will try to steal your deal.

How trading works on most blockchains today:

You stand up and shout: "I'll buy a Charizard for $50!" Now everyone hears you. The kid two tables over who has a Charizard was going to sell it for $30 — but he just heard you'll pay $50, so he raises his price. The sneaky kid next to you races to buy every Charizard in the room before you can, then flips them to you at $60. You got ripped off because everyone could hear your offer.

How Veilbook works:

You write your offer on a piece of paper — "BUY Charizard, $50" — fold it up, seal it in an envelope, and put it in a locked box on the teacher's desk. Your friend writes "SELL Charizard, $30" and does the same. Nobody in the cafeteria can see what's inside either envelope.

Now here's the magic part: the teacher doesn't open the envelopes either. Instead, a special math machine checks both envelopes while they're still sealed and says: "Yes, the buy price is higher than the sell price. This is a fair trade." The cards and money swap automatically.

The teacher (the blockchain) never saw the prices. The cafeteria (the public) never saw the prices. The sneaky kid couldn't front-run you because there was nothing to see. And neither you nor your friend had to trust anyone — the math proved the trade was fair.

That's Veilbook. Except instead of Pokemon cards, it's financial assets. Instead of envelopes, it's cryptographic commitments. And instead of a math machine, it's a zero-knowledge proof. The result is the same: a fair trade that nobody can spy on, cheat, or front-run.

Veilbook: Both Privacy and Trustlessness. No Compromise.
Veilbook is a privacy-preserving order matching engine — a fully decentralized dark pool — built on the Midnight Network.

Here's what happens when a trader submits an order:

The order stays on their machine. Price, size, direction — none of it touches the blockchain. Ever.
A cryptographic commitment goes on-chain. Think of it as a sealed envelope. The ledger knows an order exists, but not what's inside.
Tokens are escrowed atomically. The contract locks the trader's deposit — no double-spending, no broken promises.
When two orders match, a zero-knowledge proof settles them. The proof says: "These two orders are opposite directions, sizes match, and the buy price meets the sell price" — without revealing what those prices actually were.
The contract never knows the prices. The blockchain never knows the prices. Nobody knows — except the traders, after settlement.

Act 2: The Match — Proof Without Knowledge
[Match is triggered]

A zero-knowledge circuit runs locally. It opens both sealed envelopes inside the proof, verifies:

BUY price (42) >= SELL price (40) ✓
Sizes match (500 = 500) ✓
Directions are opposite (BUY ≠ SELL) ✓
The proof is submitted to the chain. The contract verifies it and atomically settles: tokens flow to buyer, tokens flow to seller. The match counter increments.

What was revealed on-chain? That the match was valid. Not the prices. Not the identities. Just: "these two commitments form a legitimate trade."

Act 3: Cancellation — Proving Ownership Without Exposure
What if Trader A changes their mind? They reconstruct the commitment locally — proving they know the original order and nonce — and the contract refunds their escrowed tokens. No one else can cancel their order because no one else knows the preimage.

Why This Is Impossible on Any Other Chain
Ethereum / Solana / Every EVM Chain
Smart contracts are fully transparent. Every state variable, every function call, every parameter is visible to every node and every block explorer. You cannot hide an order price inside a Solidity contract. Period.

Some projects try "commit-reveal" schemes, but the reveal phase exposes everything. The privacy window is temporary, and the reveal itself is front-runnable.

"Privacy Chains" (Zcash, Monero, Secret Network)
These provide transaction privacy — hiding who sent what to whom. But they cannot run arbitrary private computation. You can't write a circuit that says "compare two private prices and settle a trade" because their privacy model is fixed to transfers, not programmable logic.

Secret Network / FHE Chains
Secret Network uses Trusted Execution Environments (Intel SGX). The privacy depends on trusting hardware. Intel SGX has been broken multiple times. FHE (Fully Homomorphic Encryption) chains are years from production and orders of magnitude too slow for real-time trading.

Aztec / zkSync
These are ZK rollups — they use zero-knowledge proofs for scalability (compressing transactions), not for application-level privacy. Your smart contract state is still visible to the sequencer and provers. Aztec's Noir circuits are promising but operate within Ethereum's settlement model — they don't have a native privacy-first state model.

What Midnight Does Differently
Midnight is the only blockchain with:

The Kachina Model — A formal cryptographic framework where private state lives on the user's machine and only commitments go on-chain. This isn't a workaround; it's the protocol's foundational design.

Compact Language — A purpose-built ZK circuit language where values are private by default. You explicitly disclose() what should be public. Every other smart contract language is public by default.

Native ZK Proof Integration — Proof generation and verification are built into the consensus layer. No bolted-on circuits, no external proving services, no trust assumptions.

Witness Architecture — Private data (order details, nonces) are injected into circuits at proof time through "witnesses" that never leave the user's device. The blockchain cannot access them even in principle.

Native Token Primitives — receiveUnshielded, sendUnshielded, mintUnshieldedToken are protocol-level operations. Atomic escrow and settlement happen inside the ZK circuit execution, not as separate transactions that can be interrupted.

The result: On Midnight, the smart contract itself cannot see the private data. Not "won't" — cannot. The architecture makes it cryptographically impossible. On every other chain, the contract (or the nodes running it) must see the data to compute on it.
